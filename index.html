Low Income Communities Have More Available Opioid Clinics Than High Income Communities

By Tiara White, Craig Newmark School of Journalism

November 10, 2022


On Sunday, just around 3 pm, James Macklin,75, sat on the bench of Mosholu Park in the Bronx. Macklin was sharing a story with his friends about how he became addicted to cocaine and its detrimental effects on his life.

Macklin says," I hate to tell you, my dear, but I lost everything to crack cocaine." Macklin became homeless because of his addiction.

He continues to share his life story and speaks highly of the Bowery Mission, a well-known homeless shelter that told him he had to get clean to receive the help he needed. As a result, he became clean and is the Bowery Mission Director of Outreach for the homeless shelter, and he helps many homeless people and drug addicts who come to Bowery.

Macklin's story is one in a million, but many New Yorkers still struggle with drug addiction, especially in low-income communities.

According to the City, low-income communities like East Harlem are way more saturated with opioid clinics than in other parts of Manhattan. Statistically, low-income communities such as East Harlem have an average household income of $34,060 compared to high-income neighborhoods like Soho, whose average household income is $194,049, and Lenox Hill, whose average household income is $236,560.
